The Future Of  E-Waste

      The issue of E-waste has already manifested itself heavily in many parts of the world. The current mass of E-waste in the world is incalculable, and this mass is perpetually increasing. In regions that are heavily affected by E-waste, scientists believe that the chemical damages to the environment are irrevocable, but can be ameliorated over time. Currently, remediation efforts are being put into practice in order to decrease the amount of E-waste in heavily affected areas as well as reduce the chemical footprint left behind by E-waste.

Political Action:

  • The Basel Convention on the Control of Transboundary Movements of Hazardous Wastes and their Disposal was founded by the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) in order to aid in the fight against E-waste. Becoming a signatory of this act makes it illegal to transport dangerous materials such as E-waste between developing and developed countries. Presently, the United States is the only developed country in the world that is not a signatory of the Basel Convention, which is why the United States is a large contributor to the increasing amounts of E-waste in nations such as China and Ghana.

Manufacturers' Action

     

As the initial source of E-waste, it is the manufacturer's responsibility to make their electronics safer for when the products have reached the end of their life.

  • Devices can be created with materials that contain fewer or less harmful chemicals, which would reduce the amount of leaching that occurs in E-waste sites. Devices can also be made of biodegradable materials, allowing for the devices to become innocuous over time. In conjunction with one another, the E-waste generated by consumers can have a minimal, or even nonexistent, effect on the environment in the coming years.

An example of a newly invented biodegradable circuit board. Hover to read more! Obtained from news.illinois.edu.

     

The concept of modular technology has also been gaining interest over the years. Modular technology is the concept that if technology is composed of many interlocking, individual components, then only the malfunctioning component can be discarded instead of discarding the entire device. A prime example of this emerging field is the developing concept phone Phonebloks. Lastly, electronic manufacturers can establish take back policies, in which companies reclaim devices when they have reached the end of their lives and dispose of them properly.


     E-waste is a herculean issue that faces our society today, yet, an issue that does not receive its deserved attention. The threat of E-waste on human and environmental health is imminent, and this will not diminish unless action is taken. Luckily, options for change look promising. There are a multitude of options for change, both in politics and with technology companies. However E-waste, like most large causes, requires the involvement of society as a whole in order to make the greatest possible difference. There are many ways in which individuals can aid in the fight against E-waste on a local level. Take action today!

  • If proper actions are taken and we all work together, we can preserve the natural beauty of the Earth for many years into the future.
